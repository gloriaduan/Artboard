# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Personal art curators — art lovers, students, and designers who collect works
from museum collections for inspiration and joy. They are not on a deadline and
not at work; they're browsing for pleasure, saving pieces that catch their eye,
and arranging them into personal collage boards. The core job: **find beautiful
art, keep it, and play with how it's arranged.** Casual, personal, low-stakes —
closer to scrapbooking than to a productivity tool.

## Product Purpose

Museum Collage lets anyone browse the world's great museum collections (starting
with the Art Institute of Chicago) and build their own collage boards from what
they find. Success looks like a user returning to a board they made, rearranging
it, and feeling it's *theirs* — a personal, hand-assembled space, not a generic
gallery of bookmarks. The product turns passive museum browsing into an active,
tactile act of collection and composition.

## Brand Personality

**Playful, tactile, collage-y.** The interface should feel hands-on — pieces you
can move, layer, rotate, and arrange, like assembling a physical scrapbook or
pinboard. Voice is warm and personal, never institutional. Motion and texture
carry the personality, but the artwork always stays the hero; playfulness lives
in the *chrome and interactions*, not in competing with the images. Tactile, not
toy-like: confident and tasteful, with the charm of something hand-made.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS dashboard** — sidebar + cards + charts, Inter everywhere, blue
  accent. Corporate and templated. This is a personal space, not a workspace.
- **Sterile museum institutional site** — stiff, formal, gray, bureaucratic.
  The opposite of personal and playful.
- **Cluttered / noisy UI** — too many controls, badges, and chrome competing
  with the art. The art needs room to breathe.
- **Childish / cartoonish** — playful must not mean toy-like. Avoid bubbly
  shapes, primary-color palettes, and gimmicks. Charm comes from craft and
  tactility, not cartoon styling.

Aesthetic anchors (the *good* direction): physical collage / scrapbook
(paper, tape, overlap, rotation, hand-made imperfection) and Pinterest /
Milanote (masonry boards, drag-to-arrange, tactile movable cards).

## Design Principles

1. **The art is the hero; the UI is the frame.** Chrome recedes so works glow.
   Personality lives in interactions and texture, never in covering up the art.
2. **Make it tactile.** Arranging should feel physical — things you grab, move,
   layer, and place. Direct manipulation over forms and menus.
3. **Personal, not institutional.** Every surface should feel like the user's
   own space. Warm copy, low formality, a sense of ownership.
4. **Playful with restraint.** Charm through craft — considered motion, subtle
   texture, hand-made touches — not gimmicks or cartoon styling.
5. **Earned familiarity for the basics.** Standard, trustworthy affordances for
   browsing, saving, and navigating; save the surprise for the moments that
   reward it (placing a piece, finishing a board).

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG AA**: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, large text ≥3:1, full keyboard
navigation for browsing, saving, and board arrangement. **Motion-safe is
required** — drag/collage interactions and any reveal or layering motion must
have a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative (crossfade or instant). Tactile
features must remain operable without a pointer (keyboard equivalents for
drag-to-arrange).
